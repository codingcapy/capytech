The Importance of Projects

Posted on: November 17, 2024

Recently I got hired at a tech startup as a founding software developer.

Myself and two others form a team of three full stack devs tasked to build a generative AI web application from the ground up. Our CEO is a talented and battle-hardened UX designer who provided us wireframes to build upon. We constructed the frontend, webserver and database to create a working full stack web app that uses genAI over 9 sprints.

As my team and I got down to business and started drawing flow diagrams and ERDs on the whiteboard and churned out code, I started to realize something - virtually all the diagrams I drew and the code I wrote came from my past projects. In fact, we would regularly refer back to our past projects to bring in code snippets from them, be it API controller functions, state management functions, or database queries. I got to thinking: what if I didn't spend all that time outside of my classes to build my past projects?

Today I'm here to primarily tell you why it's so, so important to build many projects instead of just completing assignments for your course grades. That is not to say ignore your course assignments - fundamentals of programming and computer science are important, more on that later. But there is a reason why the best programs and applications were built by college dropouts.

Programming is a Craft

One thing you should never forget is that programming languages are a tool and programming is a craft. Just as performing surgery or building a chair, programming is a craft to build software that solves problems or does something the user enjoys. No matter how enticing it gets to learn about processors and ALUs and memory management, if you can't build something from what you learn, it essentially just becomes no more than a puzzle game or math problem. This is why whenever you learn something, be it Python, Java, C, HTML, CSS, JavaScript, React, Node, PHP, Flask, or C#, you should try to build something. Or several things.

Design, build, test, optimize

Engineering begins with an idea or goal. You take the idea, then you start designing the architecture for it. Then, you use the design to build it using whatever stack you choose. Then, you test, and if it breaks, you rectify. When everything is somewhat working, you optimize. You now have a fully functioning program or application.

Deployment

You might think deployment is trivial. That is not the case. You want to see your project come to fruition. You might brush that aside, shrug it off, but you will feel different when your application is actually exposed to the world. This is an experience that should not be ignored. It helps you build confidence as a software developer and makes you feel accomplished, even if you didn't make a single penny out of your project. You will come across all sorts of new problems when you actually decide to deploy your software to the world.

Celebrate, Rinse and Repeat

You've deployed your first precious application or program - Congratulations! But the journey is only beginning. This is the start of the road in an incredible journey. Like I said before, programming is a craft, but it's an incredibly powerful craft that can influence millions of people around the world, given the right idea, design, and build. You've grown as much as the project you just deployed. Now, it's time to think about the next idea, design it, build it, test it, optimize it, and release it. Or, it's time for you to add more features to your current project.

D-Day

There will come a time when you come across an incredible idea. I like to call it D-day. It may or may not come from you. It may come from a CEO of a startup. It may come from a friend or family or coworker. At this moment, you have to do your due diligence before jumping into building - are there existing designs for the application? Remember, you are a developer, not a designer. No matter how confident you are with design, there will be others who are more talented than you. If you think your design is better, go with it. Otherwise, ask for something. Then, check what the source has been doing so far regarding the product - have they begun marketing? Is there presence for it? Is the structure for success established? If none of those apply, you have to do the work yourself.

Time to Build

After you've established that there is a potential for great success from delivering something significant to users, the time will come to build. And this is the time you will realize all the apps and programs you've built so far will be your ally. Every second you spent designing, building, fixing, and optimizing, will come into play. Of course, you will run into blocks. But that's when your education will kick in - the more you understand the fundamentals of programming and computer science, the faster you will find your answer. Your experience building applications and your education will work in tandem. They will assist you in equal amounts, be it consciously or not. If you did not spend time building things from what you learned, it will take you that much longer to figure out a solution. But if you can remember running into a similar issue from a past project of yours, you are that much faster, and that much more valuable.

Build projects. Set time aside. The best chair builder didn't become the best hand-crafted chair builder by getting perfect scores in class. They built many, many chairs, and optimized over and over again by learning from their past chairs. By the way, the most expensive chair in the world costs $30 million dollars.

Let's make the world a better place.
