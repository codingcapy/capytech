Scrum

Posted on: February 9, 2023

Scrum is a highly-disciplined agile framework focusing on time-boxed iterations called sprints.

Let's start with a quick summary of the meaning of agile in tech. Agile is a philosophy and a mindset. It embraces change and continually adapts to change via constant communication and adjustments. It is a product resulting from high rates of project failures that resulted from the traditional waterfall method. Water does not flow upwards. The traditional waterfall method and software development lifecycle heavily scrutinizes a project prior to initiation via lengthy requirements engineering and analysis processes and sets a goal. It sets a concrete, rigid plan and does not deviate from that plan. Projects can take several years to complete, and often little to no feedback is taken from the customer during the project's development phase. Agile focuses on the customer's needs not just during the initiation of the project but also throughout its development. It did not begin in the tech industry and is not restricted to tech. Notable academics such as Takaeuchi and Nonaka, Highsmith, Ambler, Forrester, Sutherland, Schwaber, Beck, Cockburn, McConnell, Jeffries, and many others have built on the foundations of agile. It is constantly responsive to change and moving targets. This is not to say the waterfall method is obsolete today; many projects are more suited to be developed using the waterfall approach, especially those where the technology has matured and has been used several times in the past. Agile is best used in a bleeding edge environment where requirements are uncertain.

So what is scrum? It is a word borrowed from rugby. Its etymology derives from the rugby scrum ceremony where players periodically gather close together and strategize their next play. Scrum in tech consists of sixth ceremonies; scrum planning, sprint planning, daily scrum, backlog grooming, sprint review, and sprint retrospective. The primary objective of scrum is to constantly adapt to change via constant communication with the customer and across the team during the development of the project via these ceremonies. A scrum team only has three designated positions; a single product owner, a single scrum master, and developers. The product owner may be the customer themselves, or a specialist for the role that constantly communicates with the customer or someone representing the customer. The scrum master is a servant leader that facilitates team meetings, coaches team members and other company staff about scrum, and removes impediments. Developers may include software engineers, data architects, database administrators, UI/UX designers, testing specialists, and any team member that contributes to the output of the project. A scrum team must be less than ten members.

My curiosity began to stir as I delved deeper into the studies of the agile philosophy and the scrum framework; could this perhaps be something that could be applied on a psychological and individual level? How many individuals out there face difficulties because they struggle to adapt to change in their personal lives, or are settling in a comfort zone when they shouldn't be? Could this, perhaps, be a tool that could be used outside of the business environment and be implemented on a more personal, individual level? The more I thought about this, the more I began to realize this could be a universal hidden gem that could potentially drive my life around, and not just mine. Believe me, I made several attempts to maintain checklists, calendar deadlines and whatnot to stay ontop of tasks and stay motivated. None of them worked. They just felt ... banal? boring? I decided I should give this a try and began to test it. I knew from the get-go that I could not replicate a "pure" scrum; I was certainly not a team of several people, and I had to be my own product owner AND scrum master. But, I thought, I can deal with the latter two; I can have internal conversations on a daily basis to stay ontop of my tasks, and I can perform sprint planning, review, and retrospectives WITH myself, and I am familiar and educated with the scrum ceremonies; I can be my own coach and product owner (in the end, I knew best what my themes, epics, user stories, and user acceptance criteria were).

And so I created my own personal Trello board. I sat down for a moment and began filling up my product backlog with epics and user stories. I then prioritized them and added story points for each and added user acceptance criteria. I moved the highest priority user stories into the release backlog. And my first sprint iteration began with sprint planning.

It would be an understatement to say that implementing scrum in my life resulted in significant changes. The level of focus and motivation resulting from this framework is unrivalled. Not to mention the level of satisfaction in completing tasks. It is difficult to gauge the level of productivity during the sprint, but in retrospect, I realized I had accomplished miles more than if I had not used scrum. Mind you, I was not an unproductive person; I had daily plans to which I did my best to abide by. But there were those tasks that I would place in the backburner that I thought every day that I would get to eventually but never got to it ; scrum makes it painfully clear that it is overdue. And that is the magic of scrum; it is painful but rewarding. And you'll be thankful for it later.

Tech With Tim

Posted on: October 21, 2022

Tech with Tim is an amazing youtube channel that contains tutorials for various types projects. Tim Ruscica is a talented software developer whose tutorials are educative and highly informative. Three weeks ago Tim uploaded a video titled "How NOT To Land A Developer Job". In the video, he talks about some examples of applications to his internship post (without mentioning any names), where candidates declare that they "don't know" a certain language or are a "beginner". Tim cautions against using such language since as a recruiter he has to go through thousands of applications and is looking for candidates who are confident and "know what they're doing".

The following article discusses a phenomenon too well-known amongst software developers called Impostor Syndrome. According to the article, due to the nature of the constantly evolving tech industry, developers tend to undervalue their skills and knowledge. As I studied and developed various apps and programs over the last two years, I realized how expansive and intricate the world of tech was; multiple programming languages with each their advantages and disadvantages over each other, how they are used to achieve different tasks and different software, differenet levels of language and how close they are to human or machine language, front-end vs back-end knowledge and skills, multiple fields including data science, systems architecture, network architecture, ML, cybersecurity, and quantum computing.

As an individual who can design and build programs and software in different languages, and also as a person who loves learning new things, I personally felt excited to learn everything and anything about almost all of those fields. The more languages I learn and the more programs I build, my fascination for what a person or team can achieve with just a computer keeps on growing. Like we all did at some point, I sometimes get stuck on one single bug for days, even weeks, but eventually a solution is found, with a combination of online help and past experiences. And once you find and understand the solution, that knowledge is yours forever, and you have just become a better programmer. The key is to continue to collect solutions to different problems until they become familiar, so that when you encounter a similar problem in the future, you have an idea about how to overcome it.
